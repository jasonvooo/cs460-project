\n \n RMS\n \n Titanic\n \n \n was a British\n \n passenger liner\n \n that\n \n sank in the North Atlantic Ocean\n \n in 1912, after colliding with an\n \n iceberg\n \n during her\n \n maiden voyage\n \n from\n \n Southampton\n \n to\n \n New York City\n \n . Of the\n \n estimated 2,224 passengers and crew\n \n aboard, more than 1,500 died, making it one of modern history\'s deadliest commercial\n \n marine disasters\n \n during peacetime.\n \n RMS\n \n \n Titanic\n \n was the\n \n largest ship afloat\n \n at the time she entered service and was the second of three\n \n \n Olympic\n \n -class ocean liners\n \n operated by the\n \n White Star Line\n \n . She was built by the\n \n Harland and Wolff\n \n shipyard in\n \n Belfast\n \n .\n \n Thomas Andrews\n \n , chief naval architect of the shipyard at the time, died in the disaster.\n \n \n [4]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n was under the command of Capt.\n \n Edward Smith\n \n , who also\n \n went down with the ship\n \n . The ocean liner carried some of the wealthiest people in the world, as well as hundreds of emigrants from\n \n Great Britain and Ireland\n \n ,\n \n Scandinavia\n \n and elsewhere throughout Europe who were seeking a new life in the United States. The first-class accommodation was designed to be the pinnacle of comfort and luxury, with an on-board gymnasium, swimming pool, libraries, high-class restaurants and opulent cabins. A high-powered\n \n radiotelegraph transmitter\n \n was available for sending passenger "marconigrams" and for the ship\'s operational use.\n \n \n [5]\n \n \n Although\n \n Titanic\n \n had advanced safety features such as watertight compartments and remotely activated watertight doors, it only carried enough\n \n lifeboats\n \n for 1,178 peopleabout half the number on board, and one third of her total capacitydue to outdated maritime safety regulations. The ship carried 16 lifeboat\n \n davits\n \n which could lower three lifeboats each, for a total of 48 boats. However,\n \n Titanic\n \n carried only a total of\n \n 20 lifeboats\n \n , four of which were collapsible and proved hard to launch during the sinking.\n \n \n [6]\n \n \n

\n

\n After leaving Southampton on 10 April 1912,\n \n Titanic\n \n called at\n \n Cherbourg\n \n in France and Queenstown (now\n \n Cobh\n \n ) in Ireland before heading west to New York.\n \n \n [7]\n \n \n On 14 April, four days into the crossing and about 375 miles (600km) south of\n \n Newfoundland\n \n , she hit an iceberg at 11:40p.m. ship\'s time. The collision caused the\n \n hull\n \n plates to buckle inwards along her\n \n starboard\n \n (right) side and opened five of her sixteen watertight\n \n compartments\n \n to the sea; she could only survive four flooding. Meanwhile, passengers and some crew members were evacuated in lifeboats, many of which were launched only partially loaded. A disproportionate number of men were left aboard because of a "\n \n women and children first\n \n " protocol for loading lifeboats.\n \n \n [8]\n \n \n At 2:20a.m., she broke apart and foundered with well over one thousand people still aboard. Just under two hours after\n \n Titanic\n \n sank, the Cunard liner\n \n RMS\n \n Carpathia\n \n \n arrived and brought aboard an estimated 705 survivors.\n

\n

\n The disaster was met with worldwide shock and outrage at the huge loss of life and the regulatory and operational failures that led to it. Public inquiries in Britain and the United States led to\n \n major improvements in maritime safety\n \n . One of their most important legacies was the establishment in 1914 of the\n \n International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea\n \n (SOLAS), which still governs maritime safety. Additionally, several new wireless regulations were passed around the world in an effort to learn from the many missteps in wireless communicationswhich could have saved many more passengers.\n \n \n [9]\n \n \n

\n

\n The\n \n wreck of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n was discovered in 1985 (more than 70years after the disaster) during a US military mission,\n \n \n [10]\n \n \n and it remains on the seabed. The ship was split in two and is gradually disintegrating at a depth of 12,415 feet (3,784m). Thousands of artefacts have been recovered and displayed at museums around the world.\n \n Titanic\n \n has become one of the most famous ships in history; her memory is kept alive by numerous\n \n works of popular culture, including books, folk songs, films, exhibits, and memorials\n \n .\n \n Titanic\n \n is the second largest ocean liner wreck in the world, only beaten by her sister\n \n HMHS\n \n Britannic\n \n \n , the largest ever sunk, although she holds the record as the largest sunk while actually in service as a liner due to\n \n Britannic\n \n being used as a\n \n hospital ship\n \n at the time of her sinking. The final survivor of the sinking,\n \n Millvina Dean\n \n , aged two months at the time, died in 2009 at the age of 97.\n

\n \n Background\n \n

\n

\n The name\n \n Titanic\n \n derives from\n \n the Titan of Greek mythology\n \n . Built in Belfast, Ireland, in the\n \n United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland\n \n (as it was then known), the\n \n RMS\n \n \n Titanic\n \n was the second of the three\n \n \n Olympic\n \n -class ocean liners\n \n the first was the\n \n RMS\n \n Olympic\n \n \n and the third was the\n \n HMHS\n \n Britannic\n \n \n .\n \n \n [11]\n \n \n Britannic was originally to be called Gigantic and was to be over 1,000 feet long.\n \n \n [12]\n \n \n They were by far the largest vessels of the British shipping company\n \n White Star Line\n \n \'s fleet, which comprised 29 steamers and tenders in 1912.\n \n \n [13]\n \n \n The three ships had their genesis in a discussion in mid-1907 between the White Star Line\'s chairman,\n \n J. Bruce Ismay\n \n , and the American financier\n \n J. P. Morgan\n \n , who controlled the White Star Line\'s parent corporation, the\n \n International Mercantile Marine Co.\n \n (IMM).\n

\n

\n The White Star Line faced an increasing challenge from its main rivals\n \n Cunard\n \n , which had recently launched the\n \n \n Lusitania\n \n \n and the\n \n \n Mauretania\n \n \n the fastest passenger ships then in serviceand the German lines\n \n Hamburg America\n \n and\n \n Norddeutscher Lloyd\n \n . Ismay preferred to compete on size rather than speed and proposed to commission a new class of liners that would be larger than anything that had gone before as well as being the last word in comfort and luxury.\n \n \n [14]\n \n \n The company sought an upgrade in their fleet primarily in response to the Cunard giants but also to replace their oldest pair of passenger ships still in service, being the\n \n SS\n \n Teutonic\n \n \n of 1889 and\n \n SS\n \n Majestic\n \n \n of 1890.\n \n Teutonic\n \n was replaced by\n \n Olympic\n \n while\n \n Majestic\n \n was replaced by\n \n Titanic\n \n .\n \n Majestic\n \n would be brought back into her old spot on White Star\'s New York service after\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n loss.\n \n \n [15]\n \n \n

\n

\n The ships were constructed by the Belfast shipbuilders\n \n Harland and Wolff\n \n , who had a long-established relationship with the White Star Line dating back to 1867.\n \n \n [16]\n \n \n Harland and Wolff were given a great deal of latitude in designing ships for the White Star Line; the usual approach was for the latter to sketch out a general concept which the former would take away and turn into a ship design. Cost considerations were relatively low on the agenda and Harland and Wolff was authorised to spend what it needed on the ships, plus a five percent profit margin.\n \n \n [16]\n \n \n In the case of the\n \n Olympic\n \n -class ships, a cost of 3 million (250 million in 2015 money) for the first two ships was agreed plus "extras to contract" and the usual five percent fee.\n \n \n [17]\n \n \n

\n

\n Harland and Wolff put their leading designers to work designing the\n \n Olympic\n \n -class vessels. The design was overseen by\n \n Lord Pirrie\n \n , a director of both Harland and Wolff and the White Star Line;\n \n naval architect\n \n \n Thomas Andrews\n \n , the managing director of Harland and Wolff\'s design department; Edward Wilding, Andrews\' deputy and responsible for calculating the ship\'s design, stability and trim; and\n \n Alexander Carlisle\n \n , the shipyard\'s chief draughtsman and general manager.\n \n \n [18]\n \n \n Carlisle\'s responsibilities included the decorations, equipment and all general arrangements, including the implementation of an efficient\n \n lifeboat\n \n \n davit\n \n design.\n \n \n [a]\n \n \n

\n

\n On 29 July 1908, Harland and Wolff presented the drawings to J. Bruce Ismay and other White Star Line executives. Ismay approved the design and signed three "letters of agreement" two days later, authorising the start of construction.\n \n \n [21]\n \n \n At this point the first shipwhich was later to become\n \n Olympic\n \n had no name, but was referred to simply as "Number 400", as it was Harland and Wolff\'s four hundredth hull.\n \n Titanic\n \n was based on a revised version of the same design and was given the number 401.\n \n \n [22]\n \n \n

Her total height, measured from the base of the keel to the top of the bridge, was 104 feet (32m).\n \n \n [23]\n \n \n She measured 46,328\n \n gross register tons\n \n and with a draught of 34feet 7inches (10.54m), she displaced 52,310 tons.\n \n \n [24]\n \n \n

\n All three of the\n \n Olympic\n \n -class ships had ten decks (excluding the top of the officers\' quarters), eight of which were for passenger use. From top to bottom, the decks were:\n

  • \n \n The Boat Deck\n \n , on which the lifeboats were housed. It was from here during the early hours of 15 April 1912 that\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s lifeboats were lowered into the North Atlantic. The bridge and wheelhouse were at the forward end, in front of the captain\'s and officers\' quarters. The bridge stood 8 feet (2.4m) above the deck, extending out to either side so that the ship could be controlled while docking. The wheelhouse stood directly behind and above the bridge. The entrance to the First Class\n \n Grand Staircase\n \n and gymnasium were located midships along with the raised roof of the First Class lounge, while at the rear of the deck were the roof of the First Class smoke room and the relatively modest Second Class entrance. The wood-covered deck was divided into four segregated promenades: for officers, First Class passengers, engineers, and Second Class passengers respectively. Lifeboats lined the side of the deck except in the First Class area, where there was a gap so that the view would not be spoiled.\n \n \n [25]\n \n \n \n \n [26]\n \n \n

On\n \n Titanic\n \n , the\n \n La Carte\n \n Restaurant and the Caf Parisien provided luxury dining facilities to First Class passengers. Both were run by subcontracted chefs and their staff; all were lost in the disaster. The Second Class smoking room and entrance hall were both located on this deck. The raised forecastle of the ship was forward of the Bridge Deck, accommodating Number 1 hatch (the main hatch through to the cargo holds), numerous pieces of machinery and the anchor housings.\n \n \n [b]\n \n \n Aft of the Bridge Deck was the raised Poop Deck, 106 feet (32m) long, used as a promenade by Third Class passengers. In between were the majority of First Class cabins and the Second Class library.\n \n \n [27]\n \n \n \n \n [29]\n \n \n
    • \n

    • \n

The Third Class dining saloon was located here, as were the swimming pool,\n \n Turkish bath\n \n and kennels.\n \n \n [27]\n \n \n \n \n [31]\n \n \n \n \n [32]\n \n \n

\n \n Titanic\n \n was equipped with three main enginestwo\n \n reciprocating\n \n four-\n \n cylinder\n \n ,\n \n triple-expansion\n \n \n steam engines\n \n and one centrally placed low-pressure\n \n Parsons turbine\n \n each driving a\n \n propeller\n \n . The two reciprocating engines had a combined output of 30,000hp. The output of the steam turbine was 16,000hp.\n \n \n [23]\n \n \n The White Star Line had used the same combination of engines on an earlier liner, the\n \n SS\n \n Laurentic\n \n \n , where it had been a great success.\n \n \n [36]\n \n \n It provided a good combination of performance and speed; reciprocating engines by themselves were not powerful enough to propel an\n \n Olympic\n \n -class liner at the desired speeds, while turbines were sufficiently powerful but caused uncomfortable vibrations, a problem that affected the all-turbine Cunard liners\n \n \n Lusitania\n \n \n and\n \n \n Mauretania\n \n \n .\n \n \n [37]\n \n \n By combining reciprocating engines with a turbine, fuel usage could be reduced and motive power increased, while using the same amount of steam.\n \n \n [38]\n \n \n

\n

\n The two reciprocating engines were each 63 feet (19m) long and weighed 720 tons, with their bedplates contributing a further 195 tons.\n \n \n [37]\n \n \n They were powered by steam produced in 29 boilers, 24 of which were double-ended and five single-ended, which contained a total of 159 furnaces.\n \n \n [39]\n \n \n The boilers were 15feet 9inches (4.80m) in diameter and 20 feet (6.1m) long, each weighing 91.5 tons and capable of holding 48.5 tons of water.\n \n \n [40]\n \n \n

\n

\n They were heated by burning coal, 6,611 tons of which could be carried in\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s bunkers, with a further 1,092 tons in Hold 3. The furnaces required over 600 tons of coal a day to be shovelled into them by hand, requiring the services of 176\n \n firemen\n \n working around the clock.\n \n \n [41]\n \n \n 100 tons of ash a day had to be disposed of by ejecting it into the sea.\n \n \n [42]\n \n \n The work was relentless, dirty and dangerous, and although firemen were paid relatively generously\n \n \n [41]\n \n \n there was a high suicide rate among those who worked in that capacity.\n \n \n [43]\n \n \n

\n

\n Exhaust steam leaving the reciprocating engines was fed into the turbine, which was situated aft. From there it passed into a\n \n surface condenser\n \n , to increase the efficiency of the turbine and so that the steam could be condensed back into water and reused.\n \n \n [44]\n \n \n The engines were attached directly to long shafts which drove the propellers. There were three, one for each engine; the outer (or wing) propellers were the largest, each carrying three blades of manganese-bronze alloy with a total diameter of 23.5 feet (7.2m).\n \n \n [40]\n \n \n The middle propeller was slightly smaller at 17 feet (5.2m) in diameter,\n \n \n [45]\n \n \n and could be stopped but not reversed.\n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s electrical plant was capable of producing more power than an average city power station of the time.\n \n \n [46]\n \n \n Immediately aft of the turbine engine were four 400kW steam-driven electric generators, used to provide electrical power to the ship, plus two 30kW auxiliary generators for emergency use.\n \n \n [47]\n \n \n Their location in the stern of the ship meant they remained operational until the last few minutes before the ship sank.\n \n \n [48]\n \n \n

\n

\n Titanic lacked a searchlight in accordance with the ban on the use of searchlights in the merchant navy.\n \n \n [49]\n \n \n \n \n [50]\n \n \n

\n \n Technology\n \n

\n \n Watertight compartments and funnels\n \n

\n

\n The interiors of the\n \n Olympic\n \n -class ships were subdivided into 16 primary\n \n compartments\n \n divided by 15 bulkheads which extended well above the waterline. Eleven vertically closing watertight doors could seal off the compartments in the event of an emergency.\n \n \n [51]\n \n \n The ship\'s exposed decking was made of pine and teak, while interior ceilings were covered in painted granulated\n \n cork\n \n to combat condensation.\n \n \n [52]\n \n \n Standing above the decks were four funnels, each painted buff with black tops, (though only three were functionalthe last one was a dummy, installed for aesthetic purposes and also for kitchen ventilation)and two masts, each 155 feet (47m) high, which supported derricks for working cargo.\n

\n

\n \n Rudder and steering engines\n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s rudder was so largeat 78feet 8inches (23.98m) high and 15feet 3inches (4.65m) long, weighing over 100 tonsthat it required\n \n steering engines\n \n to move it. Two steam-powered steering engines were installed though only one was used at any one time, with the other one kept in reserve. They were connected to the short\n \n tiller\n \n through stiff springs, to isolate the steering engines from any shocks in heavy seas or during fast changes of direction.\n \n \n [53]\n \n \n As a last resort, the tiller could be moved by ropes connected to two steam\n \n capstans\n \n .\n \n \n [54]\n \n \n The capstans were also used to raise and lower the ship\'s five anchors (one port, one starboard, one in the centreline and two\n \n kedging anchors\n \n ).\n \n \n [54]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n \n \n Water, ventilation and heating\n \n

\n

\n The ship was equipped with her own waterworks, capable of heating and pumping water to all parts of the vessel via a complex network of pipes and valves. The main water supply was taken aboard while\n \n Titanic\n \n was in port, but in an emergency the ship could also distil fresh water from seawater, though this was not a straightforward process as the distillation plant quickly became clogged by salt deposits. A network of insulated ducts conveyed warm air, driven by electric fans, around the ship, and First Class cabins were fitted with additional electric heaters.\n \n \n [46]\n \n \n

\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n radiotelegraph equipment (then known as\n \n wireless telegraphy\n \n ) was leased to the White Star Line by the\n \n Marconi International Marine Communication Company\n \n , which also supplied two of its employees,\n \n Jack Phillips\n \n and\n \n Harold Bride\n \n , as operators. The service maintained a 24-hour schedule, primarily sending and receiving passenger telegrams, but also handling navigation messages including weather reports and ice warnings.\n \n \n [55]\n \n \n \n \n [56]\n \n \n \n \n [57]\n \n \n

\n The radio room was located on the Boat Deck, in the officers\' quarters. This transmitter was one of the first Marconi installations to use a rotary spark gap, which gave\n \n Titanic\n \n a distinctive musical tone that could be readily distinguished from other signals. The transmitter was one of the most powerful in the world, and guaranteed to broadcast over a radius of 350 miles (563km). An elevated\n \n T-antenna\n \n that spanned the length of the ship was used for transmitting and receiving. The normal operating frequency was 500kHz (600 m wavelength), however the equipment could also operate on the "short" wavelength of 1,000kHz (300 m wavelength) that was employed by smaller vessels with shorter antennas.\n \n \n [58]\n \n \n

\n The passenger facilities aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n aimed to meet the highest standards of luxury. According to\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s general arrangement plans, the ship could accommodate 833 First Class Passengers, 614 in Second Class and 1,006 in Third Class, for a total passenger capacity of 2,453. In addition, her capacity for crew members exceeded 900, as most documents of her original configuration have stated that her full carrying capacity for both passengers and crew was approximately 3,547. Her interior design was a departure from that of other passenger liners, which had typically been decorated in the rather heavy style of a\n \n manor house\n \n or an\n \n English country house\n \n .\n \n \n [59]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n was laid out in a much lighter style similar to that of contemporary high-class hotelsthe\n \n Ritz Hotel\n \n was a reference pointwith First Class cabins finished in the\n \n Empire style\n \n .\n \n \n [59]\n \n \n A variety of other decorative styles, ranging from the\n \n Renaissance\n \n to\n \n Louis XV\n \n , were used to decorate cabins and public rooms in First and Second Class areas of the ship. The aim was to convey an impression that the passengers were in a floating hotel rather than a ship; as one passenger recalled, on entering the ship\'s interior a passenger would "at once lose the feeling that we are on board ship, and seem instead to be entering the hall of some great house on shore".\n \n \n [60]\n \n \n

\n

\n Among the more novel features available to first-class passengers was a 7ft. deep saltwater swimming pool, a gymnasium, a\n \n squash\n \n court, and a\n \n Turkish bath\n \n which comprised\n \n electric bath\n \n , steam room, cool room, massage room, and hot room.\n \n \n [60]\n \n \n First-class common rooms were impressive in scope and lavishly decorated. They included a Lounge in the style of the\n \n Palace of Versailles\n \n , an enormous Reception Room, a men\'s Smoking Room, and a Reading and Writing Room. There was an la Carte Restaurant in the style of the\n \n Ritz Hotel\n \n which was run as a concession by the famous Italian restaurateur\n \n Gaspare Gatti\n \n .\n \n \n [61]\n \n \n A\n \n Caf Parisien\n \n decorated in the style of a French sidewalk caf, complete with ivy covered trellises and wicker furniture, was run as an annex to the restaurant. For an extra cost, first-class passengers could enjoy the finest French\n \n haute cuisine\n \n in the most luxurious of surroundings.\n \n \n [62]\n \n \n There was also a\n \n Verandah Caf\n \n where tea and light refreshments were served, that offered grand views of the ocean. At 114ft. long X 92ft. wide, the Dining Saloon on D-Deck was the largest room afloat and could seat almost 600 passengers at a time.\n \n \n [63]\n \n \n Third Class (commonly referred to as Steerage) accommodations aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n were not as luxurious as First or Second Class, but even so were better than on many other ships of the time. They reflected the improved standards which the White Star Line had adopted for trans-Atlantic immigrant and lower-class travel. On most other North Atlantic passenger ships at the time, Third Class accommodations consisted of little more than open dormitories in the forward end of the vessels, in which hundreds of people were confined, often without adequate food or toilet facilities.\n

\n

\n The White Star Line had long since broken that mould. As seen aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n , all White Star Line passenger ships divided their Third Class accommodations into two sections, always at opposite ends of the vessel from one another. The established arrangement was that single men were quartered in the forward areas, while single women, married couples and families were quartered aft. In addition, while other ships provided only open berth sleeping arrangements, White Star Line vessels provided their Third Class passengers with private, small but comfortable cabins capable of accommodating two, four, six, eight and 10 passengers.\n \n \n [64]\n \n \n

\n

\n Third Class accommodations also included their own dining rooms, as well as public gathering areas including adequate open deck space, which aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n comprised the Poop Deck at the stern, the forward and aft well decks, and a large open space on D Deck which could be used as a social hall. This was supplemented by the addition of a smoking room for men and a General Room on C Deck which women could use for reading and writing. Although they were not as glamorous in design as spaces seen in upper class accommodations, they were still far above average for the period.\n

\n

\n Leisure facilities were provided for all three classes to pass the time. As well as making use of the indoor amenities such as the library, smoking rooms, and gymnasium, it was also customary for passengers to socialise on the open deck, promenading or relaxing in hired deck chairs or wooden benches. A passenger list was published before the sailing to inform the public which members of the great and good were on board, and it was not uncommon for ambitious mothers to use the list to identify rich bachelors to whom they could introduce their marriageable daughters during the voyage.\n \n \n [65]\n \n \n

\n

\n One of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s most distinctive features was her First Class staircase, known as the\n \n Grand Staircase\n \n or Grand Stairway. Built of solid\n \n English oak\n \n with a sweeping curve, the staircase descended through seven decks of the ship, between the Boat Deck to E deck, before terminating in a simplified single flight on F Deck.\n \n \n [66]\n \n \n It was capped with a dome of wrought iron and glass that admitted natural light to the stairwell. Each landing off the staircase gave access to ornate entrance halls paneled in the\n \n William & Mary\n \n style and lit by\n \n ormolu\n \n and crystal light fixtures.\n \n \n [67]\n \n \n

\n

\n At the uppermost landing was a large carved wooden panel containing a clock, with figures of "Honour and Glory Crowning Time" flanking the clock face.\n \n \n [66]\n \n \n The Grand Staircase was destroyed during the sinking and is now just a void in the ship which modern explorers have used to access the lower decks.\n \n \n [68]\n \n \n During the filming of James Cameron\'s\n \n \n Titanic\n \n \n in 1997, his replica of the Grand Staircase was ripped from its foundations by the force of the inrushing water on the set. It has been suggested that during the real event, the entire Grand Staircase was ejected upwards through the dome.\n \n \n [69]\n \n \n

\n Although\n \n Titanic\n \n was primarily a passenger liner, she also carried a substantial amount of cargo. Her designation as a\n \n Royal Mail Ship\n \n (RMS) indicated that she carried mail under contract with the\n \n Royal Mail\n \n (and also for the\n \n United States Post Office Department\n \n ). For the storage of letters, parcels and specie (bullion, coins and other valuables), 26,800 cubic feet (760m\n \n 3\n \n ) of space in her holds was allocated. The painting\'s owner, first class passenger\n \n Mauritz Hkan Bjrnstrm-Steffansson\n \n , filed a claim for $100,000 ($2.4 million equivalent in 2014) in compensation for the loss of the artwork.\n \n \n [70]\n \n \n

\n \n Titanic\n \n was equipped with eight electric cranes, four electric winches and three steam winches to lift cargo and baggage in and out of the hold. It is estimated that the ship used some 415 tons of coal whilst in Southampton, simply generating steam to operate the cargo winches and provide heat and light.\n \n \n [74]\n \n \n

\n \n Titanic\n \n carried a total of 20 lifeboats: 14 standard wooden Harland and Wolff lifeboats with a capacity of 65people each and four Englehardt "collapsible" (wooden bottom, collapsible canvas sides) lifeboats (identified as A to D) with a capacity of 47people each. In addition, she had two emergency\n \n cutters\n \n with a capacity of 40people each.\n \n \n [75]\n \n \n \n \n [e]\n \n \n \n Olympic\n \n carried at least two collapsible boats on either side of her number one funnel.\n \n \n [76]\n \n \n \n \n [77]\n \n \n All of the lifeboats were stowed securely on the boat deck and, except for collapsible lifeboatsA and B, connected to\n \n davits\n \n by ropes. Those on the starboard side were odd-numbered 115 from bow to stern, while those on the port side were even-numbered 216 from bow to stern.\n \n \n [78]\n \n \n

\n

\n Both cutters were kept swung out, hanging from the davits, ready for immediate use, while collapsible lifeboatsC and D were stowed on the boat deck (connected to davits) immediately inboard of boats1 and 2 respectively. A and B were stored on the roof of the officers\' quarters, on either side of number1 funnel. There were no davits to lower them and their weight would make them difficult to launch by hand.\n \n \n [78]\n \n \n Each boat carried (among other things) food, water, blankets, and a spare life belt. Lifeline ropes on the boats\' sides enabled them to save additional people from the water if necessary.\n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n had 16 sets of davits, each able to handle four lifeboats. This gave\n \n Titanic\n \n the ability to carry up to 64 wooden lifeboats\n \n \n [79]\n \n \n which would have been enough for 4,000peopleconsiderably more than her actual capacity. However, the White Star Line decided that only 16 wooden lifeboats and four collapsibles would be carried, which could accommodate 1,178people, only one-third of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n total capacity. At the time, the Board of Trade\'s regulations required British vessels over 10,000tons to only carry 16lifeboats with a capacity of 990 occupants.\n \n \n [75]\n \n \n

\n

\n Therefore, the White Star Line actually provided more lifeboat accommodation than was legally required.\n \n \n [80]\n \n \n \n \n [f]\n \n \n At the time, lifeboats were intended to ferry survivors from a sinking ship to a rescuing shipnot keep afloat the whole population or power them to shore. Had the\n \n SS\n \n Californian\n \n \n responded to\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n \n distress calls\n \n , the lifeboats may have been adequate to ferry the passengers to safety as planned.\n \n \n [82]\n \n \n

\n The sheer size of\n \n Titanic\n \n and her sister ships posed a major engineering challenge for Harland and Wolff; no shipbuilder had ever before attempted to construct vessels this size.\n \n \n [83]\n \n \n The ships were constructed on Queen\'s Island, now known as the\n \n Titanic Quarter\n \n , in\n \n Belfast Harbour\n \n . Harland and Wolff had to demolish three existing\n \n slipways\n \n and build two new ones, the largest ever constructed up to that time, to accommodate both ships.\n \n \n [17]\n \n \n Their construction was facilitated by an enormous gantry built by\n \n Sir William Arrol & Co.\n \n , a Scottish firm responsible for the building of the\n \n Forth Bridge\n \n and London\'s\n \n Tower Bridge\n \n . The Arrol Gantry stood 228 feet (69m) high, was 270 feet (82m) wide and 840 feet (260m) long, and weighed more than 6,000 tons. It accommodated a number of mobile cranes. A separate floating crane, capable of lifting 200 tons, was brought in from Germany.\n \n \n [84]\n \n \n

\n

\n The construction of\n \n Olympic\n \n and\n \n Titanic\n \n took place virtually in parallel, with\n \n Olympic\n \n \n \'\n \n s keel laid down first on 16 December 1908 and\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s on 31 March 1909.\n \n \n [22]\n \n \n Both ships took about 26 months to build and followed much the same construction process. They were designed essentially as an enormous floating\n \n box girder\n \n , with the\n \n keel\n \n acting as a backbone and the frames of the hull forming the ribs. At the base of the ships, a double bottom 5feet 3inches (1.60m) deep supported 300 frames, each between 24 inches (61cm) and 36 inches (91cm) apart and measuring up to about 66 feet (20m) long. They terminated at the bridge deck (B Deck) and were covered with steel plates which formed the outer skin of the ships.\n \n \n [85]\n \n \n

\n

\n The 2,000 hull plates were single pieces of\n \n rolled\n \n steel\n \n plate\n \n , mostly up to 6 feet (1.8m) wide and 30 feet (9.1m) long and weighing between 2.5 and 3 tons.\n \n \n [86]\n \n \n Their thickness varied from 1 inch (2.5cm) to 1.5 inches (3.8cm).\n \n \n [51]\n \n \n The plates were laid in a\n \n clinkered\n \n (overlapping) fashion from the keel to the bilge. Above that point they were laid in the "in and out" fashion, where\n \n strake\n \n plating was applied in bands (the "in strakes") with the gaps covered by the "out strakes", overlapping on the edges. Commercial oxy-fuel and electric arc\n \n welding\n \n methods, ubiquitous in\n \n fabrication\n \n today, were still in their infancy; like most other iron and steel structures of the era, the hull was held together with over three million iron and steel\n \n rivets\n \n , which by themselves weighed over 1,200 tons. They were fitted using hydraulic machines or were hammered in by hand.\n \n \n [87]\n \n \n In the 1990s some material scientists concluded\n \n \n [88]\n \n \n that the steel plate used for the ship was subject to being especially brittle when cold, and that this brittleness exacerbated the impact damage and hastened the sinking. It is believed that, by the standards of the time, the steel plate\'s\n \n quality\n \n was good, not faulty, but that it was inferior to what would be used for shipbuilding purposes in later decades, owing to advances in the\n \n metallurgy\n \n of\n \n steelmaking\n \n .\n \n \n [88]\n \n \n As for the rivets, considerable emphasis has also been placed on their quality and strength.\n \n \n [89]\n \n \n \n \n [90]\n \n \n \n \n [91]\n \n \n \n \n [92]\n \n \n \n \n [93]\n \n \n

\n One of the last items to be fitted on\n \n Titanic\n \n before the ship\'s launch was her two side anchors and one centre anchor. The anchors themselves were a challenge to make with the centre anchor being the largest ever forged by hand and weighing nearly 16 tons. Twenty Clydesdale draught horses were needed to haul the centre anchor by wagon from the\n \n Noah Hingley & Sons Ltd\n \n forge shop in Netherton, near Dudley, United Kingdom to the Dudley railway station two miles away. From there it was shipped by rail to Fleetwood in Lancashire before being loaded aboard a ship and sent to Belfast.\n \n \n [94]\n \n \n

\n

\n The work of constructing the ships was difficult and dangerous. For the 15,000 men who worked at Harland and Wolff at the time,\n \n \n [95]\n \n \n safety precautions were rudimentary at best; a lot of the work was dangerous and was carried out without any safety equipment like hard hats or hand guards on machinery. As a result, deaths and injuries were to be expected. During\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s construction, 246 injuries were recorded, 28 of them "severe", such as arms severed by machines or legs crushed under falling pieces of steel. Six people died on the ship herself while she was being constructed and fitted out, and another two died in the shipyard workshops and sheds.\n \n \n [96]\n \n \n Just before the launch a worker was killed when a piece of wood fell on him.\n \n \n [97]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n was launched at 12:15p.m. on 31 May 1911 in the presence of Lord Pirrie, J. Pierpont Morgan, J. Bruce Ismay and 100,000 onlookers.\n \n \n [98]\n \n \n \n \n [99]\n \n \n 22 tons of soap and tallow were spread on the slipway to lubricate the ship\'s passage into the\n \n River Lagan\n \n .\n \n \n [97]\n \n \n In keeping with the White Star Line\'s traditional policy, the ship was not formally named or christened with champagne.\n \n \n [98]\n \n \n The ship was towed to a fitting-out berth where, over the course of the next year, her engines, funnels and superstructure were installed and her interior was fitted out.\n \n \n [100]\n \n \n

\n

\n Although\n \n Titanic\n \n was virtually identical to the class\'s lead ship\n \n Olympic\n \n , a few changes were made to distinguish both ships. The most noticeable exterior difference was that\n \n Titanic\n \n (and the third vessel in class,\n \n \n Britannic\n \n \n ) had a steel screen with sliding windows installed along the forward half of the A Deck promenade. This was installed as a last minute change at the personal request of Bruce Ismay, and was intended to provide additional shelter to First Class passengers.\n \n \n [101]\n \n \n Extensive changes were made to B Deck on\n \n Titanic\n \n as the promenade space in this deck, which had proven unpopular on\n \n Olympic\n \n was converted into additional First Class cabins, including two opulent parlour suites with their own private promenade spaces. The\n \n la Carte\n \n restaurant was also enlarged and the\n \n Caf Parisien\n \n , an entirely new feature which did not exist on\n \n Olympic\n \n , was added. These changes made\n \n Titanic\n \n slightly heavier than her sister, and thus she could claim to be the largest ship afloat. The work took longer than expected due to design changes requested by Ismay and a temporary pause in work occasioned by the need to repair\n \n Olympic\n \n , which had been in a collision in September 1911. Had\n \n Titanic\n \n been finished earlier, she might well have missed her collision with an iceberg.\n \n \n [97]\n \n \n

\n \n Sea trials\n \n

\n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n
\n \n \n
\n RMS\n \n Titanic\n \n leaving\n \n Belfast\n \n for her sea trials on 2 April 1912\n
\n
\n
\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s sea trials began at 6a.m. on Tuesday, 2 April 1912, just two days after her fitting out was finished and eight days before she was due to leave Southampton on her maiden voyage.\n \n \n [102]\n \n \n The trials were delayed for a day due to bad weather, but by Monday morning it was clear and fair.\n \n \n [103]\n \n \n Aboard were 78 stokers, greasers and firemen, and 41members of crew. No domestic staff appear to have been aboard. Representatives of various companies travelled on\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n sea trials, Thomas Andrews and Edward Wilding of Harland and Wolff and Harold A. Sanderson of IMM. Bruce Ismay and Lord Pirrie were too ill to attend.\n \n Jack Phillips\n \n and\n \n Harold Bride\n \n served as radio operators, and performed fine-tuning of the Marconi equipment. Francis Carruthers, a surveyor from the Board of Trade, was also present to see that everything worked, and that the ship was fit to carry passengers.\n \n \n [104]\n \n \n

\n

\n The sea trials consisted of a number of tests of her handling characteristics, carried out first in\n \n Belfast Lough\n \n and then in the open waters of the\n \n Irish Sea\n \n . Over the course of about 12 hours,\n \n Titanic\n \n was driven at different speeds, her turning ability was tested and a "crash stop" was performed in which the engines were reversed full ahead to full astern, bringing her to a stop in 850yd (777m) or 3 minutes and 15 seconds.\n \n \n [105]\n \n \n The ship covered a distance of about 80 nautical miles (92mi; 150km), averaging 18 knots (21mph; 33km/h) and reaching a maximum speed of just under 21 knots (24mph; 39km/h).\n \n \n [106]\n \n \n

\n

\n On returning to Belfast at about 7p.m., the surveyor signed an "Agreement and Account of Voyages and Crew", valid for 12 months, which declared the ship seaworthy. An hour later,\n \n Titanic\n \n departed Belfast to head to Southampton, a voyage of about 570 nautical miles (660mi; 1,060km). After a journey lasting about 28 hours she arrived about midnight on 4 April and was towed to the port\'s Berth 44, ready for the arrival of her passengers and the remainder of her crew.\n \n \n [107]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Maiden voyage\n \n

\n

\n Both\n \n Olympic\n \n and\n \n Titanic\n \n registered\n \n Liverpool\n \n as their home port. The offices of the White Star Line as well as Cunard were in Liverpool, and up until the introduction of the\n \n Olympic\n \n , most British ocean liners for both Cunard and White Star, such as\n \n Lusitania\n \n and\n \n Mauretania\n \n , sailed out of Liverpool followed by a port of call in\n \n Queenstown, Ireland\n \n . Since the company\'s founding in 1871, a vast majority of their operations had taken place out of Liverpool. However, in 1907 White Star established another service out of the port of Southampton on England\'s south coast, which became known as White Star\'s "Express Service". Southampton had many advantages over Liverpool, the first being its proximity to London.\n \n \n [108]\n \n \n

\n

\n In addition, Southampton, being on the south coast, allowed ships to easily cross the\n \n English Channel\n \n and make a port of call on the northern coast of France, usually at\n \n Cherbourg\n \n . This allowed British ships to pick up clientele from continental Europe before recrossing the channel and picking up passengers at Queenstown. The\n \n Southampton-Cherbourg-New York run\n \n would become so popular that most British ocean liners began using the port after\n \n World War I\n \n . Out of respect for Liverpool, ships continued to be registered there until the early 1960s.\n \n \n Queen Elizabeth 2\n \n \n was one of the first ships registered in Southampton when introduced into service by Cunard in 1969.\n \n \n [108]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n maiden voyage was intended to be the first of many trans-Atlantic crossings between Southampton and New York via Cherbourg and Queenstown on westbound runs, returning via\n \n Plymouth\n \n in England while eastbound. Indeed, her entire schedule of voyages through to December 1912 still exists.\n \n \n [109]\n \n \n When the route was established, four ships were assigned to the service. In addition to\n \n Teutonic\n \n and\n \n Majestic\n \n , the\n \n RMS\n \n Oceanic\n \n \n and the brand new\n \n RMS\n \n Adriatic\n \n \n sailed the route. When the\n \n Olympic\n \n entered service in June 1911, she replaced\n \n Teutonic\n \n , which after completing her last run on the service in late April was transferred to the Dominion Line\'s Canadian service. The following August,\n \n Adriatic\n \n was transferred to White Star\'s main Liverpool-New York service, and in November,\n \n Majestic\n \n was withdrawn from service impending the arrival of\n \n Titanic\n \n in the coming months, and was mothballed as a reserve ship.\n \n \n [110]\n \n \n \n \n [111]\n \n \n

\n

\n White Star\'s initial plans for\n \n Olympic\n \n and\n \n Titanic\n \n on the Southampton run followed the same routine as their predecessors had done before them. Each would sail once every three weeks from Southampton and New York, usually leaving at noon each Wednesday from Southampton and each Saturday from New York, thus enabling the White Star Line to offer weekly sailings in each direction. Special trains were scheduled from London and Paris to convey passengers to Southampton and Cherbourg respectively.\n \n \n [111]\n \n \n The deep-water dock at Southampton, then known as the "\n \n White Star Dock\n \n \n "\n \n , had been specially constructed to accommodate the new\n \n Olympic\n \n -class liners, and had opened in 1911.\n \n \n [112]\n \n \n

\n \n Crew\n \n

\n
\n Main article:\n \n Crew of the RMS Titanic\n \n
\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n had around 885 crew members on board for her maiden voyage.\n \n \n [113]\n \n \n Like other vessels of her time, she did not have a permanent crew, and the vast majority of crew members were casual workers who only came aboard the ship a few hours before she sailed from Southampton.\n \n \n [114]\n \n \n The process of signing up recruits had begun on 23 March and some had been sent to Belfast, where they served as a skeleton crew during\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s sea trials and passage to England at the start of April.\n \n \n [115]\n \n \n

\n

\n Captain\n \n Edward John Smith\n \n , the most senior of the White Star Line\'s captains, was transferred from\n \n Olympic\n \n to take command of\n \n Titanic\n \n .\n \n \n [116]\n \n \n \n Henry Tingle Wilde\n \n also came across from\n \n Olympic\n \n to take the post of\n \n Chief Mate\n \n .\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s previously designated Chief Mate and First Officer,\n \n William McMaster Murdoch\n \n and\n \n Charles Lightoller\n \n , were bumped down to the ranks of First and Second Officer respectively. The original Second Officer,\n \n David Blair\n \n , was dropped altogether.\n \n \n [117]\n \n \n \n \n [g]\n \n \n The\n \n Third Officer\n \n was\n \n Herbert Pitman\n \n \n MBE\n \n , the only deck officer who was not a member of the\n \n Royal Naval Reserve\n \n . Pitman was the second to last surviving officer.\n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s crew were divided into three principal departments: Deck, with 66 crew; Engine, with 325; and Victualling (pronounced\n \n vi-tal-ling\n \n ), with 494.\n \n \n [118]\n \n \n The vast majority of the crew were thus not seamen, but were either engineers, firemen, or stokers, responsible for looking after the engines, or stewards and galley staff, responsible for the passengers.\n \n \n [119]\n \n \n Of these, over 97% were male; just 23 of the crew were female, mainly stewardesses.\n \n \n [120]\n \n \n The rest represented a great variety of professionsbakers, chefs, butchers, fishmongers, dishwashers, stewards, gymnasium instructors, laundrymen, waiters, bed-makers, cleaners, and even a printer,\n \n \n [120]\n \n \n who produced a daily newspaper for passengers called the\n \n Atlantic Daily Bulletin\n \n with the latest news received by the ship\'s wireless operators.\n \n \n [55]\n \n \n \n \n [h]\n \n \n

\n

\n Most of the crew signed on in Southampton on 6 April;\n \n \n [22]\n \n \n in all, 699 of the crew came from there, and 40% were natives of the town.\n \n \n [120]\n \n \n A few specialist staff were self-employed or were subcontractors. These included the five postal clerks, who worked for the Royal Mail and the United States Post Office Department, the staff of the First Class\n \n A La Carte\n \n Restaurant and the Caf Parisien, the radio operators (who were employed by Marconi) and the eight musicians, who were employed by an agency and travelled as second-class passengers.\n \n \n [122]\n \n \n Crew pay varied greatly, from Captain Smith\'s 105 a month (equivalent to 10,200 today) to the 3 10\n \n \n s\n \n \n (340 today) that stewardesses earned. The lower-paid victualling staff could, however, supplement their wages substantially through tips from passengers.\n \n \n [121]\n \n \n

\n \n Collecting passengers\n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s maiden voyage began on Wednesday, 10 April 1912. Following the embarkation of the crew the passengers began arriving at 9:30a.m., when the\n \n London and South Western Railway\n \n \'s boat train from\n \n London Waterloo station\n \n reached\n \n Southampton Terminus railway station\n \n on the quayside, alongside\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s berth.\n \n \n [130]\n \n \n The large number of Third Class passengers meant they were the first to board, with First and Second Class passengers following up to an hour before departure. Stewards showed them to their cabins, and First Class passengers were personally greeted by Captain Smith.\n \n \n [131]\n \n \n Third Class passengers were inspected for ailments and physical impairments that might lead to their being refused entry to the United States a prospect the White Star Line wished to avoid, as it would have to carry anyone who failed the examination back across the Atlantic.\n \n \n [128]\n \n \n In all, 920 passengers boarded\n \n Titanic\n \n at Southampton 179 First Class, 247 Second Class, and 494 Third Class. Additional passengers were to be picked up at\n \n Cherbourg\n \n and\n \n Queenstown\n \n .\n \n \n [101]\n \n \n

\n

\n The maiden voyage began at noon, as scheduled. An accident was narrowly averted only a few minutes later, as\n \n Titanic\n \n passed the moored liners\n \n SS\n \n City of New York\n \n \n of the\n \n American Line\n \n and\n \n Oceanic\n \n of the White Star Line, the latter of which would have been her running mate on the service from Southampton. Her huge displacement caused both of the smaller ships to be lifted by a bulge of water and then drop into a trough.\n \n New York\n \n \n \'\n \n s mooring cables could not take the sudden strain and snapped, swinging her around stern-first towards\n \n Titanic\n \n . The incident delayed\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s departure for about an hour, while the drifting\n \n New York\n \n was brought under control.\n \n \n [133]\n \n \n

\n After making it safely through the complex tides and channels of\n \n Southampton Water\n \n and the\n \n Solent\n \n ,\n \n Titanic\n \n disembarked the Southampton\n \n pilot\n \n at the\n \n Nab Lightship\n \n and headed out into the\n \n English Channel\n \n . There, 274 additional passengers were taken aboard 142 First Class, 30 Second Class, and 102 Third Class. \n \n Atlantic crossing\n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n was planned to arrive at New York\n \n Pier 59\n \n \n \n [139]\n \n \n on the morning of 17 April.\n \n \n [140]\n \n \n After leaving Queenstown\n \n Titanic\n \n followed the Irish coast as far as\n \n Fastnet Rock\n \n ,\n \n \n [141]\n \n \n a distance of some 55 nautical miles (63mi; 102km). From there she travelled 1,620 nautical miles (1,860mi; 3,000km) along a\n \n Great Circle\n \n route across the North Atlantic to reach a spot in the ocean known as "the corner" south-east of Newfoundland, where westbound steamers carried out a change of course.\n \n Titanic\n \n sailed only a few hours past the corner on a\n \n rhumb line\n \n leg of 1,023 nautical miles (1,177mi; 1,895km) to\n \n Nantucket Shoals\n \n Light when she made her fatal contact with an iceberg.\n \n \n [142]\n \n \n The final leg of the journey would have been 193 nautical miles (222mi; 357km) to\n \n Ambrose Light\n \n and finally to\n \n New York Harbor\n \n .\n \n \n [143]\n \n \n

\n

\n From 11 April to\n \n local apparent noon\n \n the next day,\n \n Titanic\n \n covered 484 nautical miles (557mi; 896km); the following day, 519 nautical miles (597mi; 961km); and by noon on the final day of her voyage, 546 nautical miles (628mi; 1,011km). From then until the time of her sinking she travelled another 258 nautical miles (297mi; 478km), averaging about 21 knots (24mph; 39km/h).\n \n \n [144]\n \n \n

\n

\n The weather cleared as she left Ireland under cloudy skies with a headwind. Temperatures remained fairly mild on Saturday 13 April, but the following day\n \n Titanic\n \n crossed a cold\n \n weather front\n \n with strong winds and waves of up to 8 feet (2.4m). These died down as the day progressed until, by the evening of Sunday 14 April, it became clear, calm and very cold.\n \n \n [145]\n \n \n

\n

\n The first three days of the voyage from Queenstown had passed without apparent incident. A fire had begun in one of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n \n coal bunkers\n \n approximately 10 days prior to the ship\'s departure, and continued to burn for several days into its voyage,\n \n \n [146]\n \n \n but passengers were unaware of this situation. Fires occurred frequently on board steamships of that day due to\n \n spontaneous combustion\n \n of the coal.\n \n \n [147]\n \n \n The fires had to be extinguished with fire hoses, by moving the coal on top to another bunker and by removing the burning coal and feeding it into the furnace.\n \n \n [148]\n \n \n The fire was over on 14 April.\n \n \n [149]\n \n \n \n \n [150]\n \n \n There has been some speculation and discussion as to whether this fire and attempts to extinguish it may have made the ship more vulnerable to its fate.\n \n \n [151]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n received a series of warnings from other ships of drifting ice in the area of the\n \n Grand Banks of Newfoundland\n \n .\n \n \n [152]\n \n \n One of the ships to warn\n \n Titanic\n \n was the Atlantic Line\'s\n \n \n Mesaba\n \n \n .\n \n \n [153]\n \n \n Nevertheless, the ship continued to steam at full speed, which was standard practice at the time.\n \n \n [154]\n \n \n Although the ship was not trying to set a speed record,\n \n \n [155]\n \n \n timekeeping was a priority, and under prevailing maritime practices, ships were often operated at close to full speed, with ice warnings seen as advisories and reliance placed upon lookouts and the watch on the bridge.\n \n \n [154]\n \n \n It was generally believed that ice posed little danger to large vessels. Close calls with ice were not uncommon, and even head-on collisions had not been disastrous. In 1907\n \n SS\n \n Kronprinz Wilhelm\n \n \n , a German liner, had rammed an iceberg but still had been able to complete her voyage, and Captain Smith himself had declared in 1907 that he "could not imagine any condition which would cause a ship to founder. Modern shipbuilding has gone beyond that."\n \n \n [156]\n \n \n \n \n [j]\n \n \n

\n \n Sinking\n \n

\n
\n Main article:\n \n Sinking of the RMS\n \n Titanic\n \n \n
\n

\n At 11:40p.m. (\n \n ship\'s time\n \n ) on 14 April, lookout\n \n Frederick Fleet\n \n spotted an iceberg immediately ahead of\n \n Titanic\n \n and alerted the bridge.\n \n \n [159]\n \n \n \n First Officer\n \n \n William Murdoch\n \n ordered the ship to be steered around the obstacle and the engines to be stopped,\n \n \n [160]\n \n \n but it was too late; the starboard side of\n \n Titanic\n \n struck the iceberg, creating a series of holes below the waterline.\n \n \n [k]\n \n \n The hull was not punctured by the iceberg, but rather dented such that the hull\'s seams buckled and separated, allowing water to seep in. Five of the ship\'s watertight compartments were breached. It soon became clear that the ship was doomed, as she could not survive more than four compartments being flooded.\n \n Titanic\n \n began sinking bow-first, with water spilling from compartment to compartment as her angle in the water became steeper.\n \n \n [162]\n \n \n

\n

\n Those aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n were ill-prepared for such an emergency. In accordance with accepted practices of the time, where ships were seen as largely unsinkable and lifeboats were intended to transfer passengers to nearby rescue vessels,\n \n \n [163]\n \n \n \n \n [l]\n \n \n \n Titanic\n \n only had enough lifeboats to carry about half of those on board; if the ship had carried her full complement of about 3,339 passengers and crew, only about a third could have been accommodated in the lifeboats.\n \n \n [165]\n \n \n The crew had not been trained adequately in carrying out an evacuation. The officers did not know how many they could safely put aboard the lifeboats and launched many of them barely half-full.\n \n \n [166]\n \n \n Third-class passengers were largely left to fend for themselves, causing many of them to become trapped below decks as the ship filled with water.\n \n \n [167]\n \n \n The "\n \n women and children first\n \n " protocol was generally followed when loading the lifeboats,\n \n \n [167]\n \n \n and most of the male passengers and crew were left aboard.\n

\n

\n At 2:20a.m., two hours and 40 minutes after\n \n Titanic\n \n struck the iceberg, her rate of sinking suddenly increased as her forward deck dipped underwater, and the sea poured in through open hatches and grates.\n \n \n [168]\n \n \n As her unsupported stern rose out of the water, exposing the propellers, the ship began to break in two between the third and fourth funnels, due to the immense forces on the keel.\n \n \n [169]\n \n \n With the bow underwater, and air trapped in the stern, the stern remained afloat and buoyant for a few minutes longer, rising to a nearly vertical angle with hundreds of people still clinging to it,\n \n \n [170]\n \n \n before sinking. For many years it was generally believed the ship sank in one piece; however, when\n \n the wreck was located\n \n many years later, it was discovered that the ship had fully broken in two. All remaining passengers and crew were immersed into lethally cold water with a temperature of 28F (2C). Sudden immersion into freezing water typically causes death within minutes, either from\n \n cardiac arrest\n \n , uncontrollable breathing of water, or\n \n cold incapacitation\n \n (not, as commonly believed, from\n \n hypothermia\n \n ),\n \n \n [m]\n \n \n and almost all of those in the water died of cardiac arrest or other bodily reactions to freezing water, within 1530 minutes.\n \n \n [173]\n \n \n Only 13 of them were helped into the lifeboats, though these had room for almost 500 more people.\n \n \n [174]\n \n \n

\n

\n Distress signals were sent by wireless, rockets, and lamp, but none of the ships that responded was near enough to reach\n \n Titanic\n \n before she sank.\n \n \n [175]\n \n \n A radio operator on board the\n \n \n Birma\n \n \n , for instance, estimated that it would be 6a.m. before the liner could arrive at the scene. Meanwhile, the\n \n SS\n \n Californian\n \n \n , which was the last to have been in contact before the collision, saw\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s flares but failed to assist.\n \n \n [176]\n \n \n Around 4a.m.,\n \n RMS\n \n Carpathia\n \n \n arrived on the scene in response to\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s earlier distress calls.\n \n \n [177]\n \n \n

\n

\n About 710people survived the disaster and were conveyed by\n \n Carpathia\n \n to New York,\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s original destination, while at least 1,500people lost their lives.\n \n \n [113]\n \n \n \n Carpathia\n \n \'s captain described the place as an ice field that had included 20large bergs measuring up to 200 feet (61m) high and numerous smaller bergs, as well as ice floes and debris from\n \n Titanic\n \n ; passengers described being in the middle of a vast white plain of ice, studded with icebergs.\n \n \n [178]\n \n \n This area is now known as\n \n Iceberg Alley\n \n .\n \n \n [179]\n \n \n

\n \n RMS\n \n Carpathia\n \n \n took three days to reach New York after leaving the scene of the disaster. Her journey was slowed by pack ice, fog, thunderstorms and rough seas.\n \n \n [180]\n \n \n She was, however, able to pass news to the outside world by wireless about what had happened. The initial reports were confused, leading the American press to report erroneously on 15 April that\n \n Titanic\n \n was being towed to port by the\n \n SS\n \n Virginian\n \n \n .\n \n \n [181]\n \n \n

\n

\n Later that day, confirmation came through that\n \n Titanic\n \n had been lost and that most of her passengers and crew had died.\n \n \n [182]\n \n \n The news attracted crowds of people to the White Star Line\'s offices in London, New York, Montreal,\n \n \n [183]\n \n \n Southampton,\n \n \n [184]\n \n \n Liverpool and Belfast.\n \n \n [185]\n \n \n It hit hardest in Southampton, whose people suffered the greatest losses from the sinking.\n \n \n [186]\n \n \n Four out of every five crew members came from this town.\n \n \n [187]\n \n \n \n \n [n]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Carpathia\n \n docked at 9:30p.m. on 18 April at New York\'s\n \n Pier 54\n \n and was greeted by some 40,000people waiting at the quayside in heavy rain.\n \n \n [190]\n \n \n Immediate relief in the form of clothing and transportation to shelters was provided by the Women\'s Relief Committee, the\n \n Travelers Aid Society of New York\n \n , and the\n \n Council of Jewish Women\n \n , among other organisations.\n \n \n [191]\n \n \n Many of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s surviving passengers did not linger in New York but headed onwards immediately to relatives\' homes. Some of the wealthier survivors chartered private trains to take them home, and the\n \n Pennsylvania Railroad\n \n laid on a special train free of charge to take survivors to\n \n Philadelphia\n \n .\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s 214 surviving crew members were taken to the\n \n Red Star Line\n \n \'s steamer\n \n SS\n \n Lapland\n \n \n , where they were accommodated in passenger cabins.\n \n \n [192]\n \n \n

\n \n Carpathia\n \n was hurriedly restocked with food and provisions before resuming her journey to\n \n Fiume\n \n ,\n \n Austria-Hungary\n \n . Her crew were given a bonus of a month\'s wages by Cunard as a reward for their actions, and some of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s passengers joined together to give them an additional bonus of nearly 900 (88,000 today), divided among the crew members.\n \n \n [193]\n \n \n

\n The ship\'s arrival in New York led to a frenzy of press interest, with newspapers competing to be the first to report the survivors\' stories. Some reporters bribed their way aboard the\n \n pilot boat\n \n \n New York\n \n , which guided\n \n Carpathia\n \n into harbour, and one even managed to get onto\n \n Carpathia\n \n before she docked.\n \n \n [194]\n \n \n Crowds gathered outside newspaper offices to see the latest reports being posted in the windows or on billboards.\n \n \n [195]\n \n \n It took another four days for a complete list of casualties to be compiled and released, adding to the agony of relatives waiting for news of those who had been aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n .\n \n \n [o]\n \n \n

\n \n \n \n Insurance, aid for survivors and lawsuits\n \n

\n

\n In January 1912, the hulls and equipment of\n \n Titanic\n \n and\n \n Olympic\n \n had been insured through\n \n Lloyd\'s of London\n \n and\n \n London Marine Insurance\n \n . The total coverage was 1,000,000 (97,000,000 today) per ship. The policy was to be "free from all average" under 150,000, meaning that the insurers would only pay for damage in excess of that sum. The premium, negotiated by brokers Willis Faber & Company (now\n \n Willis Group\n \n ), was 15\n \n s\n \n (75\n \n p\n \n ) per 100, or 7,500 (730,000 today) for the term of one year. Lloyd\'s paid the White Star Line the full sum owed to them within 30 days.\n \n \n [197]\n \n \n

\n

\n Many charities were set up to help the victims and their families, many of whom lost their sole breadwinner, or, in the case of many Third Class survivors, everything they owned. On 29 April opera stars\n \n Enrico Caruso\n \n and\n \n Mary Garden\n \n and members of the\n \n Metropolitan Opera\n \n raised $12,000 ($300,000 in 2014)\n \n \n [198]\n \n \n in benefits for victims of the disaster by giving special concerts in which versions of "Autumn" and "Nearer My God To Thee" were part of the programme.\n \n \n [199]\n \n \n In Britain, relief funds were organised for the families of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s lost crew members, raising nearly 450,000 (44,000,000 today). One such fund was still in operation as late as the 1960s.\n \n \n [200]\n \n \n

\n

\n In the United States and Britain, more than 60 survivors combined to sue the White Star Line for damages connected to loss of life and baggage.\n \n \n [201]\n \n \n The claims totalled $16,804,112 (appr. $419 million in 2018 USD), which was far in excess of what White Star argued it was responsible for as a\n \n Limited liability company\n \n under American law.\n \n \n [202]\n \n \n Because the bulk of the litigants were in the United States, White Star petitioned the\n \n United States Supreme Court\n \n in 1914, which ruled in its favour that it qualified as an LLC and found that the causes of the ship\'s sinking were largely unforeseeable, rather than due to negligence.\n \n \n [203]\n \n \n This sharply limited the scope of damages survivors and family members were entitled to, prompting them to reduce their claims to some $2.5 million. White Star only settled for $664,000 (appr. $16.56 million in 2018), about 27% of the original total sought by survivors.\n \n \n [202]\n \n \n The settlement was agreed to by 44 of the claimants in December 1915, with $500,000 set aside for the American claimants, $50,000 for the British, and $114,000 to go towards interest and legal expenses.\n \n \n [201]\n \n \n \n \n [202]\n \n \n

\n \n Investigations into the disaster\n \n

\n
\n Main articles:\n \n United States Senate inquiry into the sinking of the RMS Titanic\n \n and\n \n British Wreck Commissioner\'s inquiry into the sinking of the RMS Titanic\n \n
\n

\n Even before the survivors arrived in New York, investigations were being planned to discover what had happened, and what could be done to prevent a recurrence. Inquiries were held in both the United States and United Kingdom, the former more robustly critical of traditions and practices, and scathing of the failures involved, and the latter broadly more technical and expert-oriented.\n \n \n [204]\n \n \n

\n

\n The\n \n U.S. Senate\'s inquiry into the disaster\n \n was initiated on 19 April, a day after\n \n Carpathia\n \n arrived in New York.\n \n \n [205]\n \n \n The chairman, Senator\n \n William Alden Smith\n \n , wanted to gather accounts from passengers and crew while the events were still fresh in their minds. Smith also needed to subpoena all surviving British passengers and crew while they were still on American soil, which prevented them from returning to the UK before the American inquiry was completed on 25 May.\n \n \n [206]\n \n \n The British press condemned Smith as an opportunist, insensitively forcing an inquiry as a means of gaining political prestige and seizing "his moment to stand on the world stage". Smith, however, already had a reputation as a campaigner for safety on U.S. railroads, and wanted to investigate any possible malpractices by railroad tycoon J. P. Morgan,\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s ultimate owner.\n \n \n [207]\n \n \n

\n

\n The\n \n British Board of Trade\'s inquiry into the disaster\n \n was headed by\n \n Lord Mersey\n \n , and took place between 2 May and 3 July. Being run by the Board of Trade, who had previously approved the ship, it was seen by some as having little interest in its own or White Star\'s conduct being found negligent.\n \n \n [208]\n \n \n

\n

\n Each inquiry took testimony from both passengers and crew of\n \n Titanic\n \n , crew members of Leyland Line\'s\n \n Californian\n \n , Captain\n \n Arthur Rostron\n \n of\n \n Carpathia\n \n and other experts.\n \n \n [209]\n \n \n The British inquiry also took far greater expert testimony, making it the longest and most detailed court of inquiry in British history up to that time.\n \n \n [210]\n \n \n The two inquiries reached broadly similar conclusions: the regulations on the number of lifeboats that ships had to carry were out of date and inadequate,\n \n \n [211]\n \n \n Captain Smith had failed to take proper heed of ice warnings,\n \n \n [212]\n \n \n the lifeboats had not been properly filled or crewed, and the collision was the direct result of steaming into a dangerous area at too high a speed.\n \n \n [211]\n \n \n

\n

\n Neither inquiry\'s findings listed negligence by IMM or the White Star Line as a factor. The American inquiry concluded that since those involved had followed standard practice the disaster was an\n \n act of God\n \n .\n \n \n [213]\n \n \n The British inquiry concluded that Smith had followed long-standing practice that had not previously been shown to be unsafe,\n \n \n [214]\n \n \n noting that British ships alone had carried 3.5 million passengers over the previous decade with the loss of just 10 lives,\n \n \n [215]\n \n \n and concluded that Smith had done "only that which other skilled men would have done in the same position". Lord Mersey did however find fault with the "extremely high speed (twenty-two knots) which was maintained" following numerous ice warnings,\n \n \n [216]\n \n \n noting that without hindsight, "what was a mistake in the case of the\n \n Titanic\n \n would without doubt be negligence in any similar case in the future".\n \n \n [214]\n \n \n

\n

\n The recommendations included strong suggestions for major changes in maritime regulations to implement new safety measures, such as ensuring that more lifeboats were provided, that lifeboat drills were properly carried out and that wireless equipment on passenger ships was manned around the clock.\n \n \n [217]\n \n \n An\n \n International Ice Patrol\n \n was set up to monitor the presence of icebergs in the North Atlantic, and maritime safety regulations were harmonised internationally through the\n \n International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea\n \n ; both measures are still in force today.\n \n \n [218]\n \n \n

\n

\n On 18 June 1912,\n \n Guglielmo Marconi\n \n gave evidence to the Court of Inquiry regarding the telegraphy. Its final report recommended that all liners carry the system and that sufficient operators maintain a constant service.\n \n \n [219]\n \n \n

\n \n Role of the SS\n \n Californian\n \n \n

\n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n
\n \n \n
\n The\n \n SS\n \n Californian\n \n \n , which had tried to warn\n \n Titanic\n \n of the danger from pack-ice\n
\n
\n
\n

\n One of the most controversial issues examined by the inquiries was the role played by\n \n SS\n \n Californian\n \n \n , which had been only a few miles from\n \n Titanic\n \n but had not picked up her distress calls or responded to her signal rockets.\n \n Californian\n \n had warned\n \n Titanic\n \n by radio of the pack ice (that was the reason\n \n Californian\n \n had stopped for the night) but was rebuked by\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s senior wireless operator,\n \n Jack Phillips\n \n .\n \n \n [220]\n \n \n

\n

\n Testimony before the British inquiry revealed that at 10:10p.m.,\n \n Californian\n \n observed the lights of a ship to the south; it was later agreed between Captain\n \n Stanley Lord\n \n and Third Officer C.V. Groves (who had relieved Lord of duty at 11:10p.m.) that this was a passenger liner.\n \n \n [220]\n \n \n At 11:50p.m., the officer had watched that ship\'s lights flash out, as if she had shut down or turned sharply, and that the port light was now visible.\n \n \n [220]\n \n \n Morse light signals to the ship, upon Lord\'s order, were made between 11:30p.m. and 1:00a.m., but were not acknowledged.\n \n \n [221]\n \n \n If\n \n Titanic\n \n was as far from the\n \n Californian\n \n as Lord claimed, then he knew, or should have known, that Morse signals would not be visible. A reasonable and prudent course of action would have been to awaken the wireless operator and to instruct him to attempt to contact\n \n Titanic\n \n by that method. Had Lord done so, it is possible he could have reached\n \n Titanic\n \n in time to save additional lives.\n \n \n [82]\n \n \n

\n

\n Captain Lord had gone to the chartroom at 11:00p.m. to spend the night;\n \n \n [222]\n \n \n however, Second Officer Herbert Stone, now on duty, notified Lord at 1:10a.m. that the ship had fired five rockets. Lord wanted to know if they were company signals, that is, coloured flares used for identification. Stone said that he did not know and that the rockets were all white. Captain Lord instructed the crew to continue to signal the other vessel with the Morse lamp, and went back to sleep. Three more rockets were observed at 1:50a.m. and Stone noted that the ship looked strange in the water, as if she were\n \n listing\n \n . At 2:15a.m., Lord was notified that the ship could no longer be seen. Lord asked again if the lights had had any colours in them, and he was informed that they were all white.\n \n \n [223]\n \n \n

\n

\n \n Californian\n \n eventually responded. At around 5:30a.m., Chief Officer George Stewart awakened wireless operator\n \n Cyril Furmstone Evans\n \n , informed him that rockets had been seen during the night, and asked that he try to communicate with any ship. He got news of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s loss, Captain Lord was notified, and the ship set out to render assistance. She arrived well after\n \n Carpathia\n \n had already picked up all the survivors.\n \n \n [224]\n \n \n

\n

\n The inquiries found that the ship seen by\n \n Californian\n \n was in fact\n \n Titanic\n \n and that it would have been possible for\n \n Californian\n \n to come to her rescue; therefore, Captain Lord had acted improperly in failing to do so.\n \n \n [225]\n \n \n \n \n [q]\n \n \n

\n \n Survivors and victims\n \n

\n
\n Main article:\n \n Passengers of the RMS Titanic\n \n
\n

\n The number of casualties of the sinking is unclear, due to a number of factors. These include confusion over the passenger list, which included some names of people who cancelled their trip at the last minute, and the fact that several passengers travelled under aliases for various reasons and were therefore double-counted on the casualty lists.\n \n \n [227]\n \n \n The death toll has been put at between 1,490 and 1,635people.\n \n \n [228]\n \n \n The tables below use figures from the British\n \n Board of Trade\n \n report on the disaster.\n \n \n [113]\n \n \n While the use of Marconi wireless system did not achieve the result of bringing a rescue ship to the\n \n Titanic\n \n before it sank, the use of wireless did bring the\n \n Carpathia\n \n in time to rescue some of the survivors who otherwise would have perished due to exposure.\n \n \n [229]\n \n \n

\n

\n The water temperature in the area where\n \n Titanic\n \n sank, which was well below normal, also contributed to the rapid death of many passengers during the sinking. Water temperature readings taken around the time of the accident were reported to be 28F (2C). Typical water temperatures were normally in the mid-40F range during mid-April.\n \n \n [230]\n \n \n The coldness of the water was a critical factor, often causing death within minutes for many of those in the water.\n

\n

\n Fewer than a third of those aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n survived the disaster. Some survivors died shortly afterwards; injuries and the effects of exposure caused the deaths of several of those brought aboard\n \n Carpathia\n \n .\n \n \n [231]\n \n \n The figures show stark differences in the survival rates of the different classes aboard\n \n Titanic\n \n . Although only 3% of first-class women were lost, 54% of those in third class died. Similarly, five of six first-class and all second-class children survived, but 52 of the 79 in third class perished. The differences by gender were even bigger: nearly all female crew members, first and second class passengers were saved. Men from the First Class died at a higher rate than women from the Third Class.\n \n \n [232]\n \n \n In total, 50% of the children survived, 20% of the men and 75% of the women.\n

\n

\n The last living survivor,\n \n Millvina Dean\n \n from England, who at only nine weeks old was the youngest passenger on board, died aged 97 on 31 May 2009.\n \n \n [233]\n \n \n Two special survivors were the stewardess\n \n Violet Jessop\n \n and the stoker Arthur John Priest,\n \n \n [234]\n \n \n who survived the sinkings of both\n \n Titanic\n \n and\n \n \n HMHSBritannic\n \n \n and were aboard\n \n \n RMS Olympic\n \n \n when she was rammed in 1911.\n \n \n [235]\n \n \n \n \n [236]\n \n \n \n \n [237]\n \n \n

\n \n Retrieval and burial of the dead\n \n

\n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n
\n \n \n

\n Once the massive loss of life became known, White Star Line chartered the cable ship\n \n CS\n \n Mackay-Bennett\n \n \n from\n \n Halifax, Nova Scotia\n \n ,\n \n Canada\n \n , to retrieve bodies.\n \n \n [238]\n \n \n Three other Canadian ships followed in the search: the cable ship\n \n Minia\n \n ,\n \n \n [239]\n \n \n lighthouse supply ship\n \n Montmagny\n \n and\n \n sealing vessel\n \n \n Algerine\n \n .\n \n \n [240]\n \n \n Each ship left with embalming supplies, undertakers, and clergy. Of the 333 victims that were eventually recovered, 328 were retrieved by the Canadian ships and five more by passing North Atlantic steamships.\n \n \n [241]\n \n \n \n \n [r]\n \n \n

\n

\n The first ship to reach the site of the sinking, the CS\n \n Mackay-Bennett\n \n , found so many bodies that the embalming supplies aboard were quickly exhausted. Health regulations required that only embalmed bodies could be returned to port.\n \n \n [243]\n \n \n Captain Larnder of the\n \n Mackay-Bennett\n \n and undertakers aboard decided to preserve only the bodies of first class passengers, justifying their decision by the need to visually identify wealthy men to resolve any disputes over large estates. As a result, many third class passengers and crew were buried at sea. Larnder identified many of those buried at sea as crew members by their clothing, and stated that as a mariner, he himself would be contented to be buried at sea.\n \n \n [244]\n \n \n

\n

\n Bodies recovered were preserved for transport to Halifax, the closest city to the sinking with direct rail and steamship connections. The Halifax coroner,\n \n John Henry Barnstead\n \n , developed a detailed system to identify bodies and safeguard personal possessions. Relatives from across North America came to identify and claim bodies. A large temporary morgue was set up in the\n \n curling\n \n rink of the\n \n Mayflower Curling Club\n \n and undertakers were called in from all across eastern Canada to assist.\n \n \n [244]\n \n \n Some bodies were shipped to be buried in their home towns across North America and Europe. About two-thirds of the bodies were identified. Unidentified victims were buried with simple numbers based on the order in which their bodies were discovered. The majority of recovered victims, 150bodies, were buried in three Halifax cemeteries, the largest being\n \n Fairview Lawn Cemetery\n \n followed by the nearby\n \n Mount Olivet\n \n and\n \n Baron de Hirsch\n \n cemeteries.\n \n \n [245]\n \n \n

\n

\n In mid-May 1912,\n \n RMS\n \n Oceanic\n \n \n recovered three bodies over 200 miles (320km) from the site of the sinking who were among the original occupants of CollapsibleA. When Fifth Officer\n \n Harold Lowe\n \n and six crewmen returned to the wreck site sometime after the sinking in a lifeboat to pick up survivors, they rescued a dozen males and one female from CollapsibleA, but left the dead bodies of three of its occupants.\n \n \n [s]\n \n \n After their retrieval from CollapsibleA by\n \n Oceanic\n \n , the bodies were buried at sea.\n \n \n [246]\n \n \n

\n

\n The last\n \n Titanic\n \n body recovered was steward James McGrady, Body No. 330, found by the chartered Newfoundland sealing vessel\n \n Algerine\n \n on 22 May and buried at\n \n Fairview Lawn Cemetery\n \n in Halifax on 12 June.\n \n \n [247]\n \n \n

\n

\n Only 333 bodies of\n \n Titanic\n \n victims were recovered, one in five of the over 1,500 victims. Some bodies sank with the ship while currents quickly dispersed bodies and wreckage across hundreds of miles making them difficult to recover. By June, one of the last search ships reported that life jackets supporting bodies were coming apart and releasing bodies to sink.\n \n \n [248]\n \n \n

\n \n Wreck\n \n

\n
\n Main article:\n \n Wreck of the RMS Titanic\n \n
\n
\n
\n \n \n \n
\n
\n \n \n
\n The bow of the wrecked RMS\n \n Titanic\n \n , photographed in June 2004\n

\n \n Titanic\n \n was long thought to have sunk in one piece and, over the years, many schemes were put forward for raising the wreck. None came to fruition.\n \n \n [249]\n \n \n The fundamental problem was the sheer difficulty of finding and reaching a wreck that lies over 12,000 feet (3,700m) below the surface, in a location where the water pressure is over 6,500 pounds per square inch (450bar).\n \n \n [250]\n \n \n A number of expeditions were mounted to find\n \n Titanic\n \n but it was not until 1 September 1985 that a Franco-American expedition led by\n \n Robert Ballard\n \n succeeded.\n \n \n [251]\n \n \n \n \n [252]\n \n \n

\n

\n The team discovered that\n \n Titanic\n \n had in fact split apart, probably near or at the surface, before sinking to the seabed. The separated bow and stern sections lie about a third of a mile (0.6km) apart in\n \n Titanic Canyon\n \n off the coast of Newfoundland. They are located 13.2 miles (21.2km) from the inaccurate coordinates given by\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s radio operators on the night of her sinking,\n \n \n [253]\n \n \n and approximately 715 miles (1,151km) from Halifax and 1,250 miles (2,012km) from New York.\n

\n

\n Both sections struck the sea bed at considerable speed, causing the bow to crumple and the stern to collapse entirely. The bow is by far the more intact section and still contains some surprisingly intact interiors. In contrast, the stern is completely wrecked; its decks have pancaked down on top of each other and much of the hull plating was torn off and lies scattered across the sea floor. The much greater level of damage to the stern is probably due to structural damage incurred during the sinking. Thus weakened, the remainder of the stern was flattened by the impact with the sea bed.\n \n \n [254]\n \n \n

\n

\n The two sections are surrounded by a debris field measuring approximately 5 by 3 miles (8.0km 4.8km).\n \n \n [255]\n \n \n It contains hundreds of thousands of items, such as pieces of the ship, furniture, dinnerware and personal items, which fell from the ship as she sank or were ejected when the bow and stern impacted on the sea floor.\n \n \n [256]\n \n \n The debris field was also the last resting place of a number of\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'\n \n s victims. Most of the bodies and clothes were consumed by sea creatures and bacteria, leaving pairs of shoes and bootswhich have proved to be inedibleas the only sign that bodies once lay there.\n \n \n [257]\n \n \n

\n

\n Since its initial discovery, the wreck of\n \n Titanic\n \n has been revisited on numerous occasions by explorers, scientists, filmmakers, tourists and salvagers, who have recovered thousands of items from the debris field for conservation and public display. The ship\'s condition has deteriorated significantly over the years, particularly from accidental damage by submersibles but mostly because of an accelerating rate of growth of iron-eating bacteria on the hull.\n \n \n [258]\n \n \n In 2006, it was estimated that within 50 years the hull and structure of\n \n Titanic\n \n would eventually collapse entirely, leaving only the more durable interior fittings of the ship intermingled with a pile of rust on the sea floor.\n \n \n [259]\n \n \n

\n Many artefacts from\n \n Titanic\n \n have been recovered from the sea bed by RMS Titanic Inc., which exhibits them in touring exhibitions around the world and in a permanent exhibition at the\n \n Luxor Las Vegas\n \n hotel and casino in\n \n Las Vegas\n \n ,\n \n Nevada\n \n .\n \n \n [260]\n \n \n A number of other museums exhibit artefacts either donated by survivors or retrieved from the floating bodies of victims of the disaster.\n \n \n [261]\n \n \n

\n

\n On 16 April 2012, the day after the 100th anniversary of the sinking, photos were released showing possible human remains resting on the ocean floor. The photos, taken by\n \n Robert Ballard\n \n during an expedition led by\n \n NOAA\n \n in 2004, show a boot and a coat close to\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n stern which experts called "compelling evidence" that it is the spot where somebody came to rest, and that human remains could be buried in the sediment beneath them.\n \n \n [262]\n \n \n The wreck of the\n \n Titanic\n \n falls under the scope of the 2001\n \n UNESCO Convention on the Protection of the Underwater Cultural Heritage\n \n . This means that all states party to the convention will prohibit the pillaging, commercial exploitation, sale and dispersion of the wreck and its artefacts. Because of the location of the wreck in\n \n international waters\n \n and the lack of any exclusive jurisdiction over the wreckage area, the convention provides a state co-operation system, by which states inform each other of any potential activity concerning ancient shipwreck sites, like the\n \n Titanic\n \n , and co-operate to prevent unscientific or unethical interventions.\n \n \n [263]\n \n \n \n \n [264]\n \n \n \n \n [265]\n \n \n

\n After the disaster, recommendations were made by both the British and American Boards of Inquiry stating that ships should carry enough lifeboats for all aboard, mandated lifeboat drills would be implemented, lifeboat inspections would be conducted, etc. Many of these recommendations were incorporated into the\n \n International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea\n \n passed in 1914.\n \n \n [266]\n \n \n The convention has been updated by periodic amendments, with a completely new version adopted in 1974.\n \n \n [267]\n \n \n Signatories to the convention followed up with national legislation to implement the new standards. For example in Britain, new "Rules for Life Saving Appliances" were passed by the\n \n Board of Trade\n \n on 8 May 1914 and then applied at a meeting of British steamship companies in Liverpool in June 1914.\n \n \n [268]\n \n \n

\n

\n Further, the United States government passed the\n \n Radio Act of 1912\n \n . This act, along with the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea, stated that radio communications on passenger ships would be operated 24 hours a day, along with a secondary power supply, so as not to miss distress calls. Also, the Radio Act of 1912 required ships to maintain contact with vessels in their vicinity as well as coastal onshore radio stations.\n \n \n [269]\n \n \n In addition, it was agreed in the International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea that the firing of red rockets from a ship must be interpreted as a sign of need for help. Once the Radio Act of 1912 was passed it was agreed that rockets at sea would be interpreted as distress signals only, thus removing any possible misinterpretation from other ships.\n \n \n [269]\n \n \n

\n

\n Finally, the disaster led to the formation and international funding of the\n \n International Ice Patrol\n \n , an agency of the United States Coast Guard that to the present day monitors and reports on the location of North Atlantic Ocean icebergs that could pose a threat to transatlantic sea traffic. Coast Guard aircraft conduct the primary reconnaissance. In addition, information is collected from ships operating in or passing through the ice area. Except for the years of the two World Wars, the International Ice Patrol has worked each season since 1913. During the period there has not been a single reported loss of life or property due to collision with an iceberg in the patrol area.\n \n \n [270]\n \n \n In 1912, the\n \n Board of Trade\n \n \n chartered\n \n the\n \n barque\n \n \n \n Scotia\n \n \n to act as a\n \n weather ship\n \n in the\n \n Grand Banks of Newfoundland\n \n , keeping a look-out for icebergs. A\n \n Marconi\n \n \n wireless\n \n was installed to enable her to communicate with stations on the coast of\n \n Labrador\n \n and\n \n Newfoundland\n \n .\n \n \n [271]\n \n \n \n \n [272]\n \n \n

\n \n Titanic\n \n has gone down in history as the ship that was called unsinkable.\n \n \n [t]\n \n \n For more than 100 years, she has been the inspiration of fiction and non-fiction. She is commemorated by monuments for the dead and by museums exhibiting artefacts from the wreck. Just after the sinking, memorial postcards sold in huge numbers\n \n \n [273]\n \n \n together with memorabilia ranging from tin candy boxes to plates, whiskey jiggers,\n \n \n [274]\n \n \n and even black mourning teddy bears.\n \n \n [275]\n \n \n Several survivors wrote books about their experiences,\n \n \n [276]\n \n \n but it was not until 1955 that the first historically accurate book \n \n \n A Night to Remember\n \n \n was published.\n \n \n [277]\n \n \n

\n

\n The first film about the disaster,\n \n \n Saved from the Titanic\n \n \n , was released only 29 days after the ship sank and had an actual survivor as its starthe silent film actress\n \n Dorothy Gibson\n \n .\n \n \n [278]\n \n \n The British film\n \n \n A Night to Remember\n \n (1958)\n \n is still widely regarded as the most historically accurate movie portrayal of the sinking.\n \n \n [279]\n \n \n The most financially successful by far has been\n \n James Cameron\n \n \'s\n \n \n Titanic\n \n \n (1997), which became the highest-grossing film in history up to that time,\n \n \n [280]\n \n \n as well as the winner of 11\n \n Oscars\n \n at the\n \n 70th Academy Awards\n \n , including\n \n Best Picture\n \n and\n \n Best Director\n \n for Cameron.\n \n \n [281]\n \n \n

\n

\n The\n \n Titanic\n \n disaster was commemorated through a variety of memorials and monuments to the victims, erected in several English-speaking countries and in particular in cities that had suffered notable losses. These included Southampton, Liverpool and Belfast in the United Kingdom; New York and Washington, D.C. in the United States; and Cobh (formerly Queenstown) in Ireland.\n \n \n [282]\n \n \n A number of museums around the world have displays on\n \n Titanic\n \n ; the most prominent is in\n \n Belfast\n \n , the ship\'s birthplace (see\n \n below\n \n ).\n

\n

\n RMS Titanic Inc., which is authorised to salvage the wreck site, has a permanent\n \n Titanic\n \n exhibition at the\n \n Luxor Las Vegas\n \n hotel and casino in\n \n Nevada\n \n which features a 22-ton slab of the ship\'s hull. It also runs an exhibition which travels around the world.\n \n \n [283]\n \n \n In Nova Scotia, Halifax\'s\n \n Maritime Museum of the Atlantic\n \n displays items that were recovered from the sea a few days after the disaster. They include pieces of woodwork such as panelling from the ship\'s First Class Lounge and an original deckchair,\n \n \n [284]\n \n \n as well as objects removed from the victims.\n \n \n [285]\n \n \n In 2012 the centenary was marked by plays, radio programmes, parades, exhibitions and special trips to the site of the sinking together with commemorative stamps and coins.\n \n \n [186]\n \n \n \n \n [286]\n \n \n \n \n [287]\n \n \n \n \n [288]\n \n \n \n \n [289]\n \n \n

\n

\n In a frequently commented-on literary coincidence,\n \n Morgan Robertson\n \n authored a novel called\n \n \n Futility\n \n \n in 1898 about a fictional British passenger liner with the plot bearing a number of similarities to the\n \n Titanic\n \n disaster. In the novel the ship is the SS\n \n Titan\n \n , a four-stacked liner, the largest in the world and considered unsinkable. And like the\n \n Titanic\n \n , she sinks after hitting an iceberg and does not have enough lifeboats.\n \n \n [290]\n \n \n

\n \n In Northern Ireland\n \n

\n

\n Only recently has the significance of\n \n Titanic\n \n most notably been given by\n \n Northern Ireland\n \n where it was built by\n \n Harland and Wolff\n \n in the capital city,\n \n Belfast\n \n . While the rest of the world embraced the glory and tragedy of\n \n Titanic\n \n , in its birth city,\n \n Titanic\n \n remained a taboo subject throughout the 20th century. The sinking brought tremendous grief and was a blow to the city\'s pride. But its shipyard was also a place many Catholics regarded as hostile.\n \n \n [291]\n \n \n In the latter half of the century, during a 30-year sectarian conflict\n \n Titanic\n \n was a reminder of the lack of civil rights that in part contributed towards\n \n the Troubles\n \n . While the fate of Titanic remained a well-known story within local households throughout the 20th century, commercial investment around RMS\n \n Titanic\n \n \n \'s\n \n legacy was modest because of these issues.\n \n \n [292]\n \n \n

\n

\n After the Troubles and\n \n Good Friday Agreement\n \n , the number of overseas tourists visiting Northern Ireland dramatically increased to 30 million (100% rise by 2008).\n \n \n [293]\n \n \n It was subsequently identified in the Northern Ireland Tourism Board\'s Strategic Framework for Action 20042007 that the significance of and interest in\n \n Titanic\n \n globally (partly due to the\n \n 1997 film\n \n Titanic\n \n \n ) was not being fully exploited as a tourist attraction.\n \n \n [294]\n \n \n Thus,\n \n Titanic\n \n Belfast was spearheaded, along with some smaller projects, such as a\n \n \n Titanic\n \n memorial\n \n .\n \n \n [295]\n \n \n

\n

\n In 2012 on the ship\'s centenary,\n \n \n Titanic\n \n Belfast\n \n visitor attraction was opened on the site of the shipyard where\n \n Titanic\n \n was built.\n \n \n [296]\n \n \n It was Northern Ireland\'s second most visited tourist attraction with almost 700,000 visitors in 2016.\n \n \n [297]\n \n \n

\n

\n Despite over\n \n 1,600 ships\n \n being built by\n \n Harland and Wolff\n \n in Belfast Harbour, Queen\'s Island became renamed after its most famous ship,\n \n Titanic Quarter\n \n in 1995. Once a sensitive story,\n \n Titanic\n \n is now considered one of Northern Ireland\'s most iconic and uniting symbols.\n \n \n [298]\n \n \n

\n

\n In late August 2018, several groups were vying for the right to purchase the 5,500\n \n Titanic\n \n relics that were an asset of the bankrupt\n \n Premier Exhibitions\n \n .\n \n \n [299]\n \n \n Eventually,\n \n \n Titanic\n \n Belfast\n \n , Titanic Foundation Limited and the\n \n National Museums Northern Ireland\n \n joined with the\n \n National Maritime Museum\n \n as a consortium that was raising money to purchase the 5,500 artefacts. The group intended to keep all of the items together as a single exhibit. Oceanographer\n \n Robert Ballard\n \n said he favored this bid since it would ensure that the memorabilia would be permanently displayed in Belfast (where the Titanic was built) and in\n \n Greenwich\n \n .\n \n \n [299]\n \n \n The museums were critical of the bid process set by the Bankruptcy court in Jacksonville, Florida. The minimum bid for the 11 October 2018 auction was set at US$21.5 million (16.5m) and the consortium did not have enough funding to meet that amount.\n \n \n [300]\n \n \n \n \n [301]\n \n \n On 17 October 2018,\n \n \n The New York Times\n \n \n reported that a consortium of three\n \n hedge funds\n \n \n \n Apollo Global Management\n \n , Alta Fundamental Advisers, and PacBridge Capital Partnershad paid US$19.5 million for the collection.\n \n \n [302]\n \n \n

\n There have been several proposals and studies for a project to build a\n \n replica ship\n \n based on the Titanic. A project by South African businessman Sarel Gaus was abandoned in 2006, and a project by Australian businessman\n \n Clive Palmer\n \n was announced in 2012, known as the\n \n \n Titanic II\n \n \n .\n

\n

\n A Chinese shipbuilding company known as Wuchang Shipbuilding Industry Group Co., Ltd commenced construction in January 2014 to build\n \n a replica ship of the\n \n Titanic\n \n \n for use in a resort. The vessel will house many features of the original, such as a ballroom, dining hall, theatre, first-class cabins, economy cabins and swimming pool.\n \n \n [310]\n \n \n \n \n [311]\n \n \n Tourists will be able to reside inside the\n \n Titanic\n \n during their time at the resort. It will be permanently docked at the resort and feature an audiovisual simulation of the sinking, which has caused some criticism.\n \n \n [312]\n \n \n

\n

\n The RMS\n \n Olympic\n \n was the sister ship of the\n \n Titanic\n \n . The interior decoration of the dining salon and the grand staircase were in identical style and created by the same craftsmen. Large parts of the interior of the\n \n Olympic\n \n were later sold and are now in the\n \n White Swan Hotel, Alnwick\n \n , which gives an impression of how the interior of the\n \n Titanic\n \n looked.\n
